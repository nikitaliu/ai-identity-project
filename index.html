AI, Language & Identity:
Who Are We Becoming?

A UCLA sociology student argues that AI writing tools don't just help non-native speakers communicate — they quietly reshape the voice behind the words, pushing users toward an invisible standard nobody chose.

Interview with Tom Liu, Undergraduate, Sociology, UCLA | Theory: W.E.B. Du Bois & John Stuart Mill | Reading time: 8 min

WHEN TOM LIU runs his academic essays through an AI writing tool, something happens that he can't quite name. The sentences get cleaner. The argument lands more sharply. But reading it back, he wonders: is this still mine?

Liu, a sociology student at UCLA born in Chengdu, China, has spent the past year thinking carefully about what AI does to language — not just to grammar and clarity, but to identity. His answer draws on W.E.B. Du Bois and John Stuart Mill to make a case that is as much philosophical as personal.

"It sounds better," he says. "But it doesn't sound like me." The interview below explores what that gap means — and why it might matter more than any of us realize.

Q1 Does AI change how you express yourself? +
Yeah, honestly it does. When I write something and then run it through ChatGPT to 'clean it up,' it sounds more polished — but it doesn't quite sound like me anymore. There's this strange tension: I want to be understood clearly, but I also want people to know it's actually me speaking. It reminds me of Du Bois' double consciousnessDouble ConsciousnessThe sense of always looking at oneself through the eyes of others — a psychological tension between one's identity and the identity imposed by society.— W.E.B. Du Bois, The Souls of Black Folk (1903) — this constant awareness of measuring your own voice against how others will receive it. The AI becomes a kind of internal audience. You write, it rewrites, and gradually you start writing for the rewrite.

Q2 Do you feel pressured to use AI in your academic writing? +
It's not like anyone forces you. But there's an invisible expectation — especially in academic settings. If everyone around you is producing polished, AI-assisted work, and yours looks 'raw' or uneven, it stands out in a bad way. You conform without being told to. Mill would call this social tyrannySocial TyrannySociety exercises a tyranny "more formidable than many kinds of political oppression" through collective opinion — because it leaves fewer escape routes than law.— John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859) — pressure that operates not through law or explicit rules, but through collective opinion and silent judgment. The scary part is how natural it feels. You don't experience it as pressure. You experience it as just... trying to do well.
social tyrannyconformityMill
Q3 How does your experience as a non-native speaker shape this? +
It amplifies everything. As a non-native speaker, I'm already hyper-aware of how my English sounds. There's always this two-nessTwo-ness"Two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings" — inhabiting two selves simultaneously. Not weakness, but the product of navigating a world that demands legibility before authenticity.— W.E.B. Du Bois, The Souls of Black Folk (1903) — the voice inside my head, which thinks in Mandarin and structures ideas in a certain rhythm, and the voice I produce in English, which I'm constantly monitoring and adjusting. AI gives me a tool to close that gap. But it also makes the gap feel more real, more necessary to close. Before AI, I could tell myself: this is just how I write. Now there's always a 'better version' available.

Q4 What do you actually lose when AI 'improves' your writing? +
The accent. Not literally — but the rhythm of how I think. The way I structure ideas is shaped by my first language, by my culture, by the specific path my mind has traveled. When AI smooths that out, it's more efficient, more readable. But something authentic disappears. It's like wearing a mask that makes people trust you more — and after a while, you start to forget what your own face looks like. The polish is real. But so is the loss.
identityauthenticitylanguage
Q5 Is this conformity harmful, or is it just smart adaptation? +
That's the hard question. I don't think adaptation is inherently bad. But Mill makes a distinction I keep coming back to: between growing through genuine engagement with the world, and being shaped by external pressure without awareness. The danger isn't using AI. The danger is using AI without realizing it's changing you. When that awareness disappears, so does your ability to push back. You lose individualityIndividualityThe development of one's own faculties through genuine engagement — not mere eccentricity. A person who lets the world choose their plan of life "has no need of any other faculty than the ape-like one of imitation."— John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859) — not because anyone took it, but because you handed it over one edit at a time.
individualityMillconformity
Q6 What would you want AI tools to do differently? +
Ask what I actually meant. Right now, most AI tools work by replacing — they take your words and offer a cleaner version. What if instead they asked: "what were you trying to say here?" That would be a tool that expands you rather than replaces you. You have to see the veil before you can decide what to do about it. Most people never get there. They just accept the rewrite.

Double Consciousness

The sense of always looking at oneself through the eyes of others — a psychological tension between one's own identity and the identity imposed by society. Du Bois described it as "measuring one's soul by the tape of a world that looks on in amused contempt and pity."

The Souls of Black Folk (1903)

"Two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings." Inhabiting two selves simultaneously — not as weakness, but as the product of navigating a world that demands legibility before it grants belonging.

The Veil

The invisible barrier that distorts how marginalized people see themselves — always filtered through the distorting lens of dominant society first. Seeing through the veil means recognizing that distortion, not eliminating it.

Social Tyranny

Society exercises a tyranny "more formidable than many kinds of political oppression" through collective opinion and social pressure. Unlike law, it leaves fewer escape routes — it penetrates daily life and shapes the soul itself, without a visible enforcer.

Individuality

The Connection

AI does not force anyone to write differently. It simply offers a better version — constantly, frictionlessly. That is exactly the mechanism Mill feared: not the threat of punishment, but the seamless availability of the approved form.

Analysis

What the interview reveals

Tom Liu is not opposed to AI. He is opposed to using it without awareness.

What Du Bois called the veil — the distorting lens through which marginalized people are forced to see themselves — has a contemporary counterpart. For non-native speakers navigating English-language institutions in the age of AI, that lens is the algorithm that always knows how to say it better.

What Mill called social tyranny has found a new delivery mechanism. AI does not demand conformity. It merely makes conformity seamless. One edit at a time. One rewrite at a time.

The question is not whether to use these tools. The question is whether you can see the veil they create — and choose, with full awareness, what to do about it.

Most people never reach that question. The rewrite is offered. It is accepted. The voice shifts. Gradually, without announcement, a different person speaks.

This project does not just analyze AI — it demonstrates how theory lives inside real experience.
