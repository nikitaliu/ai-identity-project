AI, Language & Identity:
Who Are We Becoming?

A UCLA sociology student argues that AI writing tools don't just help non-native speakers communicate — they quietly reshape the voice behind the words, pushing users toward an invisible standard nobody chose.

Interview with Tom Liu, Undergraduate, Sociology, UCLA | Theory: W.E.B. Du Bois & John Stuart Mill | Reading time: 8 min

WHEN TOM LIU runs his academic essays through an AI writing tool, something happens that he can't quite name. The sentences get cleaner. The argument lands more sharply. But reading it back, he wonders: is this still mine?

Liu, a sociology student at UCLA born in Chengdu, China, has spent the past year thinking carefully about what AI does to language — not just to grammar and clarity, but to identity. His answer draws on W.E.B. Du Bois and John Stuart Mill to make a case that is as much philosophical as personal.

"It sounds better," he says. "But it doesn't sound like me." The interview below explores what that gap means — and why it might matter more than any of us realize.

Analysis

What the interview reveals

Tom Liu is not opposed to AI. He is opposed to using it without awareness.

What Du Bois called the veil — the distorting lens through which marginalized people are forced to see themselves — has a contemporary counterpart. For non-native speakers navigating English-language institutions in the age of AI, that lens is the algorithm that always knows how to say it better.

What Mill called social tyranny has found a new delivery mechanism. AI does not demand conformity. It merely makes conformity seamless. One edit at a time. One rewrite at a time.

The question is not whether to use these tools. The question is whether you can see the veil they create — and choose, with full awareness, what to do about it.

Most people never reach that question. The rewrite is offered. It is accepted. The voice shifts. Gradually, without announcement, a different person speaks.

This project does not just analyze AI — it demonstrates how theory lives inside real experience.
